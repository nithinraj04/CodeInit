The Playfair Cipher

The Playfair cipher is a classical symmetric encryption method that transforms plaintext into ciphertext, making it unreadable to anyone without the decryption key. Renowned for its simplicity yet effectiveness, the Playfair cipher played a pivotal role in secure communication during pivotal historical moments, from military operations to diplomatic exchanges.

About the Cipher

The Playfair cipher was the first practical digraph substitution cipher. This scheme was invented in 1854 by Charles Wheatstone, but was named after Lord Playfair who promoted the use of this cipher. In Playfair cipher, unlike traditional cipher, we encrypt a pair of alphabets ie, diagraphs instead of a single alphabet.

So, how does this work?

The algorithm has 2 steps, which are, 5x5 Key Matrix Generation and the Actual Encryption. The matrix generation is simple. The first unique letters of the 'key' will fill the initial matrix entires, in the relative order of their occurrence in the key. Then, the remaining of the 26 alphabets are filled in alphabetic order. You may notice a problem here, that is the number of alphabets is 26 while the total number of elements in matrix is 25. Hence, to make the algorithm work, we drop a letter, which is usually either i or j.

Now, how exactly does the 'actual encryption' take place?

Initially, we have to pair two adjacent letters in the message. Each pair is called diagraph. Same letters cannot be paired. So, the message must be modified accordingly. To do this, add a bogus letter (say ‘x’) in one of the letter's place. Now, the length of the message, after satisfying above condition, must be of even parity. Otherwise, to make it even, add another letter (usually ‘z’) at the last position of the message to complete the pairing.

Once the above two conditions are satisfied, we proceed to the encryption. The encryption process is governed by 3 rules which are as follows:

  • If both elements of a digraph belong to a same row in the key matrix, their corresponding encryption will be the element immediately to the right of each element (wraps around as well).
  • If both elements of a digraph belong to a same column in the key matrix, their corresponding encryption will be the element immediately below each element (wraps around as well).
  • Else, the two elements will form the diagonally opposite corners of a rectangle. Then their encryption will be the elements in other two corners, taken horizontally.

This will be the key-matrix formed from our key 'MANGO', and the digraph formed from our to be encrypted text, 'apple' will be AP-PL-EZ.
As A and P are neither in same row nor in the same column, the letters belonging to the corners of the rectangle formed by A and P, paired horizontally, are taken for encryption.
Now, as P and L belong to the same row, the letters to the immediate right of these letters are taken for encryption.
Finally, as E and Z are neither in same row nor in same column, the letters belonging to corners of the rectangle formed by E and Z, paired horizontally, are taken for encryption.
