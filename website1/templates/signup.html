About the Cypher

The Playfair cipher was the first practical digraph substitution cipher. The scheme was invented in 1854 by Charles Wheatstone but was named after Lord Playfair who promoted the use of the cipher. In Playfair cipher unlike traditional cipher we encrypt a pair of alphabets(digraphs) instead of a single alphabet.

So, how does this work?

The algorithm has 2 steps,which are, 5x5 Key matrix generation and the actual encryption. The matrix generation is simple. The first few letters of the matrix will be the unique letters of the “key”, in the relative order of their occurrence in the key. Then, the remaining alphabets are filled in alphabetic order. You may notice a problem here the number of alphabets is 26 while the total number of elements in matrix is 25. Hence, to make the algorithm work, we drop a letter, which is usually either i or j

Now, how exactly does the “actual encryption” works?

Firstly, the text to be encrypted is modified so as to be compatible with the algorithm. This involves two things • For the purpose of encryption, we have to pair two adjacent letters in the message. Each pair is called diagraph. Same letters cannot be paired. So, the message must be modified accordingly. To do this, add a bogus letter (say ‘x’) in place of one of them. • The length of the message, after satisfying above condition, must be of even parity. To make it even, add a bogus letter (usually ‘z’) at the last position of the message to complete the pair

Once the above two conditions are satisfied, we proceed to the encryption. the encryption process is governed by 3 rules which are as follows:

  • If both elements of a digraph belong to a same row in the key matrix, their corresponding encryption will be the element immediately to the right of each element (wraps around as well).
  • If both elements of a digraph belong to a same column in the key matrix, their corresponding encryption will be the element immediately below each element (wraps around as well).
  • Else, the two elements will form the diagonally opposite corners of a rectangle. Then their encryption will be the elements in other two corners, taken horizontally.
